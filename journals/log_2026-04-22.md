- **Robustness is strong**: adversarial-/die-conditioned content does not
  meaningfully hurt return. The policy tolerates confabulated imagery.
- **Steerability is weak**: flipping the imagination (via 180°-rotated obs
  sent to Gemini) only changes the argmax movement in 4-12% of cases, vs
  40-84% when flipping the obs directly. Imagination moves the policy, but
  not directionally.
- **Horizon shape**: hidden-flip rate rises 3% → 7% → 3% → 12% from steps 0
  → 75 → 150 → 300. Not monotone, but the end of the horizon shows the
  clearest reading. More data (more eps × more intervention steps) would
  stabilize this curve.

### What this closes

The imagination branch in Track A:
- reads content (constant-embed < real),
- but reads it as an undifferentiated embedding-shape feature, not as a
  direction- / goal-specific forecast,
- produces tolerance to confabulation (robustness) but no steerability,
- is at parity with full-response on return (no performance lift from the
  predonly change).

This is the **policy-side bottleneck signature**. Fidelity could still add
something — that's what Track B/C tests — but if it plateaus, the architecture
/ training signal is the ceiling, not the prediction quality.

## Track A validation on held-out shards — CORRECTED

Earlier in this file I reported val_acc=0.907 and Δ=+0.807 from an
"oracle-full" val slice and called it memorization. That framing was wrong.
Here is the real story (caught during Track B/C validation):

- **Bug**: the `track_a_predonly_awr.sh` and `track_a_predonly_freezenone.sh`
  scripts passed `ORACLE_DATA=predict_only_final_v2_cadence5_gemini_emb/...`
  as `--val-data`. That file has `hidden_state` embedded from the **full
  response** of the predict_only oracle Gemini pipeline (State Understanding
  + Prediction). Track A was trained on the **predonly-suffix** embedding of
  regular-obs concise Gemini. Completely different hidden distribution. So
  val_acc=0.907 / Δ=+0.807 are on a hidden distribution the policy never
  trained against; the numbers are artifacts, not evidence of memorization
  or content-sensitivity.
- **Same bug affected Track B/C**: their scripts used the *predonly* oracle
  file. That is a closer match for Track C (oracle-at-label-time) but still
  not a matched val for Track A (non-oracle) or Track B (non-oracle,
  thinking). Those 10% AWR val numbers reflect the distribution mismatch,
  not a training problem.
- **Correct held-out val** — each freezenone checkpoint evaluated on the
  last 3 files of its own training directory (same prompt, same embedder):

| Policy | acc_real | Δ(zero−real) NLL | Δ(shuffled−real) NLL |
|---|---|---|---|
| Track A predonly (freezenone) | 38.86% | **+0.142** | **+0.003** |
| Track B thinking (freezenone) | 48.41% | +0.089 | +0.085 |
| Track C grounded (freezenone) | **49.04%** | **+0.191** | **+0.195** |

Reading Δ(shuffled−real) is the content-specificity test (does the policy
distinguish the *correct* content from a shuffled-same-file content, not
just "hidden present" vs zero):

- Track A has +0.003 on shuffled → it reads *presence* of hidden but not
  *content*. Matches the in-distribution direction-CF finding (2.7% hidden-
  flip rate).
- Track B has +0.085 ≈ +0.089 on zero → genuine content-specific.
- Track C has +0.195 ≈ +0.191 on zero → strongest content-specific signal,
  and largest overall Δ. This is the first real signal that the fidelity
  axis moves the needle on content-reading.

This revises the "policy-side bottleneck" read from the in-distribution
probe: the architecture *can* learn content-specific reading when the
labels carry enough fidelity (Track C). What the non-oracle policies (A, B)
couldn't do wasn't fundamental — it was a *labels-too-noisy-to-use* problem.

Whether this translates to online *performance* is decided by the 50-ep
eval (in flight now). What it definitively unblocks is **steerability** —
Track C has a hidden branch whose content is actionable.

Track A (non-oracle). Track B (thinking, non-oracle) sits in between.

Ordering on *steerability* proxy: **C > B > A**.

Caveat: each was validated on its *own* held-out files, so absolute
accuracy differences are partly about distribution hardness, not policy
quality. The Δ metrics are the comparable numbers.

|---|---|---|

### Inversion — fidelity raises content-reading but hurts return

Track C has the strongest content-*specific* reading (Δ_shuf=+0.195) and
the *worst* online return (~14.7). Track A has the weakest content-specific
reading (+0.026) and the best return (~18-19). Clean inversion.

**Hypothesis**: Track C's content-specificity was learned against oracle
labels (Gemini saw the 5-step future). At deploy time, Gemini gets only the
current obs — non-oracle content, different distribution. The policy's
learned content-specific wiring now interprets non-oracle content as if it
were oracle; this likely produces confidently-wrong actions. The held-out
content-reading test can't see this because held-out is still oracle
distribution. This was a *predicted* failure mode for the experiment (see
Apr-21 "grounded eval path (a) vs (b)" discussion), just quantified now.

### Axes read

- **Steerability** (content-reading per Δ_shuf and hidden-CF): grounded
  raises this noticeably over non-oracle; thinking raises it modestly.
- **Performance** (online return): fidelity does not lift return; grounded
  actively hurts via train/eval mismatch.
- **Generalization**: corroborated — held-out (in-distribution) reading
  differs from OOD performance; grounded's gain on in-distribution doesn't
  transfer to OOD inference.
- **Robustness**: pending (die/adversarial probes queued for B/C).
- **Exploration / Verification**: untouched.

## In-distribution probe for B/C (and top-2M A)

Ran `tools/in_distribution_semantic_probe.py` (500 samples, no Gemini calls,
just forward-pass counterfactuals on training-distribution states) for the
three top-2M freezenone checkpoints plus the Track A full freezenone for
reference:

| Policy | obs_flip | obs_cf | hidden_cf | hidden_perm | KL hidden_cf |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Track A predonly **FULL** | 79.4% | 17.4% | 2.7% | 6.4% | 0.010 |
| Full-response FULL (Apr-19) | 93.8% | 35.1% | 6.3% | 7.0% | 0.042 |
| Track A predonly **top-2M** | 90.6% | 36.9% | **13.7%** | 16.8% | 0.129 |
| Track B thinking top-2M | 83.0% | 46.5% | 11.6% | 15.8% | 0.134 |
| Track C grounded top-2M | 86.2% | 41.7% | 11.1% | 15.0% | **0.242** |

### Corrections to earlier in-distribution read

1. My earlier framing "Track A has fundamental content-reading failure
   because hidden_cf (2.7%) < hidden_perm (6.4%)" was measured on Track A
   **full**. The same policy trained on **top-2M** has hidden_cf=13.7% — 5×
   higher. The Apr-21 size-ablation finding already predicted this shape
   ("hidden sensitivity climbs as data shrinks"); I missed the connection
   when analyzing the full probe.
2. At matched data size (top-2M), A/B/C all have hidden_cf ~11-14%, and all
   three are slightly below their own hidden_perm rate (15-17%). None of
   the three policies differentiates "real other state's hidden" from
   "permuted version of this state's hidden" in a way that flips the
   argmax more often.
3. What distinguishes Track C: **KL on hidden swap = 0.242**, 2× higher
   than A/B. The policy's full action distribution shifts more when hidden
   is swapped, even when the argmax doesn't flip more often. This is the
   distribution-level analog of the held-out Δ(shuf−real)=+0.195 finding.
   Grounded labels produce *distribution*-level hidden-utilization without
   additional argmax-flip sensitivity.

### Revised overall read of the fidelity axis

- Content-specific reading (distributional, via KL or Δ_shuf): grounded
  demonstrably higher than thinking or predonly. **Fidelity moves this.**
- Content-specific reading (decision-level, via argmax-flip rate): all three
  similar at matched size. **Fidelity does not move this.**
- Online return: A > B > C at matched size. **Fidelity hurts via
  train/eval mismatch.**

A cleaner conclusion: grounded training teaches the policy to *weight* the
hidden branch more (higher KL per swap, higher held-out NLL gap), but that
additional weight is *not* translated into different argmax decisions nor
into online return gains. Consistent with "grounded learned to trust
content it doesn't get at deploy time, so the trust mechanism misfires."

## In-distribution probe — LANDED

`tools/in_distribution_semantic_probe.py` ran on 500 offline states from
`final_trajectories_psf_v2_cadence5_predonly_gemini_emb/trajectories_000000.npz`
(14s on L40S; no Gemini calls needed). Also ran on the Apr-19 full-response
freezenone for comparison.

| Probe | Track A predonly | Full-response | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| obs_flip_180 (argmax change) | 79.4% | 93.8% | — |
| obs_cf (random state's obs) | 17.4% | 35.1% | — |
| **hidden_cf (random state's hidden)** | **2.7%** | **6.3%** | 2.3× |
| hidden_perm (dims shuffled) | 6.4% | 7.0% | — |
| KL(A || hidden_cf) | 0.010 | 0.042 | 4× |
| ΔV hidden_cf | +0.030 | −0.017 | — |

### Reading — this is the cleanest policy-side evidence yet

1. **Hidden-CF rate ≈ obs-CF rate ÷ 5.5** in both policies. The obs branch
   dominates by ~5-6×. The hidden branch is essentially subsidiary.
2. **Track A predonly hidden-CF (2.7%) is LOWER than dim-permutation (6.4%).**
   Swapping the hidden with one from a genuinely different training state
   changes the movement argmax *less* than randomly permuting its dimensions.
   This means the policy does not differentiate real hiddens from each other
   meaningfully; it treats them as near-constant with some noise.
3. **Full-response hidden-CF (6.3%) ≈ dim-permutation (7.0%).** Slightly
   better than predonly, but still at the permutation-noise floor.
4. KL on hidden-CF: 0.01 nats (predonly) / 0.04 nats (full-response). At
   this magnitude the output distribution is essentially unchanged when the
   hidden is swapped.

**Updated read (after Track C held-out val landed, see section below):**
this is a *training-signal* content-reading failure, not a fundamental
*architecture* one. Track C (oracle-at-label-time) under the same
architecture shows Δ(shuffled−real)=+0.195 NLL on its held-out val —
actual content-specific reading. So the architecture *can* learn content
when the labels carry enough fidelity; Track A/B's non-oracle labels didn't.
This also is not an OOD generalization failure: in-distribution Track A
still doesn't read content, so the issue predates any OOD shift.

### Predonly reads hidden LESS than full-response

Despite our intuition that "cleaner forecast-only signal = easier to use",
predonly sits at 2.7% hidden-CF while full-response is at 6.3%. Hypothesis:
the State Understanding section in full-response added cross-state variance
that the policy latched onto even if only weakly. Removing it narrowed the
embedding distribution so the policy had even less signal to use.

This makes Track C (grounded) even more decisive: if training with
upper-bound fidelity predictions still can't push hidden-CF above the
permutation-noise floor, the path to steerability is architecture / training
signal, not content quality.

### Axis impact
- **Generalization**: ruled out as primary failure mode.
- **Steerability**: fundamentally capped by hidden-branch usage pattern.
- **Robustness**: explained — the policy is robust to content because it
  barely uses content.
- Next lever candidates: gated residual for hidden, explicit
  intervention-based CF loss at train time, or learned contrast pairs
  (verification axis too).

## Disentangling embedding-quality vs policy-reading

The HP/Food/Drink perturbation probe gives a clean separation of the two.
For each Gemini call, we swap one intrinsic field (Health/Food/Drink) and
re-embed, then forward through the policy:

- **Embedding response (cosine sim of real vs perturbed hidden)**: 0.90-0.93
  on Track A predonly. The embedding genuinely shifts when we perturb a
  single intrinsic — gemini-embedding-001 IS encoding the difference.
- **Policy response (argmax change)**: 8.5-9.1% — basically at the null
  rate. |ΔV| 0.13-0.19 (small, direction-incoherent). food_low gives
  +0.011 (wrong sign — should be negative since low food is dangerous).

So the embedding-quality layer is doing its job (90% cos sim on real-vs-
perturbed is a 10% shift, plenty of signal), but the policy-architecture
layer discards it. That's the policy-side bottleneck, isolated.

Compare to Apr-17 HP perturb on phase1_gemini_emb (pre-fix, different
recipe): food_low ΔV = -0.33 (correct sign, 30x stronger). The pre-v2
full-response gemini_emb policy at least moved in the right direction on
intrinsics; Track A predonly has collapsed this signal.
